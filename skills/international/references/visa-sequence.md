# The F-1 visa sequence — from deposit to campus

Every link in this chain depends on the previous one, and the slowest links
(document verification, embassy wait times) are outside the family's control.
Start the day the enrollment deposit goes in — typically by the **May 1** national
reply date. Primary source for the whole sequence:
[travel.state.gov — Student Visa](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/study/student-visa.html).
Where a step lives on a college's side, the college's international student
office page is the contract.

## Step 0 — Deposit at exactly one college

Deposit, then immediately find the college's **international student office**
(sometimes "ISSS" or "OISS") page and its I-20 request instructions. This office,
not admissions, runs the rest of the college-side process.

## Step 1 — Request the Form I-20

The college issues **Form I-20** (Certificate of Eligibility) after verifying:

- the passport identity page — the name on the I-20 must match the passport
  **exactly**; mismatches cause problems at every later step;
- financial evidence — typically proof of funds covering at least the first year
  (bank statements, sponsor letters, the certification of finances already
  prepared during the application → `financial-aid`, `international-aid.md`).

When the I-20 arrives (often as a signed PDF), check every field: name spelling,
date of birth, program start date, funding amounts. Errors are fixable now and
painful later.

## Step 2 — SEVIS registration happens on the college's side

Issuing the I-20 creates the student's record in **SEVIS**, the US government
database tracking international students. The **SEVIS ID** (starts with "N")
is printed at the top of the I-20 — it's needed for the next two steps.

## Step 3 — Pay the SEVIS I-901 fee at fmjfee.com

Pay at [fmjfee.com](https://www.fmjfee.com) using the SEVIS ID and school code
from the I-20. The F-1 fee is typically US$350 — verify the current amount on the
site. Pay **before** the visa interview and keep the printed receipt; electronic
payments post quickly, but don't leave it to the last day.

## Step 4 — Complete the DS-160

The **DS-160** is the online nonimmigrant visa application, filed on the
Department of State's site (linked from travel.state.gov). It needs a photo
meeting the official specifications and takes longer than expected — budget an
evening. Print the confirmation page with the barcode. Separately, pay the visa
application (MRV) fee per the local embassy's instructions — typically US$185 for
F visas; the embassy's page states the current amount and payment method.

## Step 5 — Book the embassy/consulate interview immediately

**This is the step that strands people.** Appointment wait times vary from days
to months depending on the post — check the wait-time tool on
[travel.state.gov](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/study/student-visa.html)
for the specific embassy. Book the earliest slot the moment the DS-160 and fees
are done; some posts allow rebooking to an earlier slot if one opens.

## Step 6 — Interview day: the document checklist

Per travel.state.gov, bring:

- passport valid for travel to the US (typically valid at least six months beyond
  the period of stay; country-specific agreements can exempt this — verify);
- DS-160 confirmation page;
- application fee payment receipt (if payment was required before the interview);
- photo (if the DS-160 photo upload failed);
- **Form I-20**, signed.

Also commonly requested — bring even if not listed as mandatory:

- SEVIS I-901 fee receipt;
- admission/offer letter;
- academic records and test scores;
- evidence of funds (the same financial documents from Step 1);
- evidence of ties home / intent to depart after the program.

## Step 7 — Issuance and the two timing rules

- An F-1 visa for a new student can be issued up to **365 days before the program
  start date** on the I-20 — so there is no reason to wait.
- The student may **not enter the US more than 30 days before** that program
  start date. Both rules: travel.state.gov (link above).

If the case goes into "administrative processing" (Form 221(g)), issuance can
take additional weeks — one more reason the interview happens early. A refusal
under 214(b) is not permanent; the student can reapply with a stronger showing
of finances or ties.

**Canada/Bermuda note:** citizens of Canada and Bermuda typically do not need a
visa stamp to study, but still need the I-20 and the paid I-901 fee at the border
— verify on travel.state.gov.

## Step 8 — Travel and arrival

- Carry in **hand luggage**, never checked: passport with visa, I-20, I-901 and
  financial documents, admission letter.
- Arrive within the 30-day window (for fall starts, typically late July–August).
- Within the first days on campus, complete the international office's
  **check-in** so the school can activate the SEVIS record. Full-time enrollment
  is a condition of F-1 status from day one.

## Common failure modes

| Failure | Prevention |
|---|---|
| Name on I-20 ≠ passport | Send a passport scan with the I-20 request; proofread the I-20 on arrival |
| Interview booked late | Book the same week as the deposit-and-I-20 request; check wait times first |
| Bank statements too old | Ask the college how recent statements must be before requesting the I-20 |
| Arriving >30 days early or after the start date | Book flights only after the visa is issued; keep inside the window |
| I-20 program start date wrong | Check it against the college's actual first day; ask the DSO to correct |
