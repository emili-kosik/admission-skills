# Standardized Testing in 2026–27: Required, Optional, or Ignored

Testing policy is the part of admissions that changed most since 2020 — and then changed back. This chapter tells you where things stand for the 2026–27 cycle (applying fall 2026), how to read the three policy types, and how to schedule tests so a registration deadline never ambushes you.

## The landscape: requirements are back at many selective schools

From 2020 to roughly 2023, almost every US college was test-optional. Starting in 2024, many of the most selective schools re-required the SAT or ACT, citing internal research that scores helped them find talent. The result is a split landscape:

| Policy | What it means | Schools (as of this writing — always verify) |
|---|---|---|
| **Test-required** | You must submit an SAT or ACT score to be considered | Harvard, Yale, Dartmouth, Brown, Cornell, Penn, Stanford, MIT, Caltech, Georgetown, Johns Hopkins, among others |
| **Test-optional** | You choose whether to submit; the file is read either way | Columbia, Princeton (both currently test-optional — check each school's stated end date); 2,000+ others — see the maintained list at [fairtest.org](https://fairtest.org) |
| **Test-free / test-blind** | Scores are not considered even if you send them | The University of California and Cal State systems ([admission.universityofcalifornia.edu](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu)) |

Two things to hold onto:

- **Policies are set per college, per year.** The table above reflects announced policies for this cycle, but schools revise them. Before you finalize your list, check each college's own admissions page — never a third-party summary, including this one.
- **Scores do not change what a reach is.** A perfect SAT does not turn a sub-15% admit-rate school into a match. Those schools are reaches for everyone; a strong score just keeps you in the conversation.

## What each policy means for strategy

- **Any test-required school on your list → you are testing.** Plan sittings early enough to allow one retake (most students improve on a second attempt).
- **Test-optional schools:** submit if your score is at or above the school's middle-50% range (find it in the college's Common Data Set or on [College Scorecard](https://collegescorecard.ed.gov), built on [nces.ed.gov](https://nces.ed.gov) data). Below the 25th percentile, withholding is usually the better call. Optional means genuinely optional — schools admit plenty of non-submitters — but a good score is an asset you control.
- **Test-free schools (UC/CSU):** don't send scores; they won't be read for admission. If your list is UC-only, you may reasonably skip the SAT/ACT entirely. Most students have mixed lists, so most students still test.
- **Mixed list (the common case):** test as if for your required schools, then decide submission school-by-school for the optional ones.

## Test dates and registration lead times

**SAT** — typically eight Saturday administrations per year, following an **August, September, October, November, December, March, May, June** pattern. Registration typically closes about **two weeks before** the test date (a late window runs a few days longer; allow ~30 days if you need to borrow a testing device). Scores usually arrive about two weeks after you test. Exact dates and deadlines: [satsuite.collegeboard.org/sat/dates-deadlines](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/sat/dates-deadlines).

**ACT** — typically seven national dates per year, following a **September, October, December, February, April, June, July** pattern. Regular registration closes about **five weeks before** the test — much earlier than the SAT, and the most common way students get locked out. Scores typically arrive two to four weeks after. Exact dates: [act.org](https://www.act.org/content/act/en/products-and-services/the-act/registration/test-dates.html).

**PSAT/NMSQT** — administered in **October** through your high school (you register with the school, not College Board). The 11th-grade sitting is the National Merit qualifier. Details: [satsuite.collegeboard.org/psat-nmsqt](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/psat-nmsqt/test-dates).

**AP exams** — the **first two full weeks of May**, scores in early July. Calendar: [apstudents.collegeboard.org/exam-calendar](https://apstudents.collegeboard.org/exam-calendar).

### Which sitting feeds which deadline

| Application deadline | Last comfortable SAT | Last comfortable ACT |
|---|---|---|
| Nov 1 ED/EA (remember: ED is **binding**) | October | October (September is safer) |
| Jan 1–15 Regular Decision / ED2 | December | December |

Later sittings sometimes squeak in — some colleges accept scores that arrive after the deadline — but that is a per-college policy you must confirm on the college's official page, not a plan.

## A sane testing timeline

- **10th grade, October:** PSAT practice sitting if your school offers it. No prep pressure.
- **11th grade, October:** PSAT/NMSQT (National Merit counts this year).
- **11th grade, winter:** start prep. **Khan Academy's Official SAT Practice is free** and built with College Board — [khanacademy.org](https://www.khanacademy.org/sat) — including full-length adaptive practice in the same Bluebook app used on test day. Paid prep is optional, not required.
- **11th grade, spring (March–June):** first real SAT or ACT sitting.
- **12th grade, August–October:** one retake if you want it, in time for early deadlines.

Front-loading to junior spring means senior fall is a retake, not a first attempt — that alone removes most testing panic.

## English proficiency tests (international applicants)

If English is not your native language or language of instruction, most US colleges require TOEFL, IELTS, or the Duolingo English Test. Score **delivery** time is the trap — colleges need the score in hand, not just taken:

| Test | Typical results timing | Verify at |
|---|---|---|
| TOEFL iBT | ~3–4 days to your account | [ets.org/toefl](https://www.ets.org/toefl/test-takers/ibt/scores/get-scores.html) |
| IELTS | ~1–5 days computer, ~13 days paper | [ielts.org](https://ielts.org) |
| Duolingo English Test | ~2 days | [englishtest.duolingo.com](https://englishtest.duolingo.com) |

Rules of thumb: test by **September–October** for November 1 ED/EA deadlines, and by **October–November** for January deadlines, leaving margin for official delivery to each college. Not every school accepts every test (Duolingo acceptance in particular varies), and minimum scores differ — check each college's international-applicant page. Many schools waive the requirement if you've studied in English for several years; the waiver terms are also per-college.

## Bottom line

1. Sort your list into required / optional / test-free using each college's own admissions page (the [fairtest.org](https://fairtest.org) list is a good starting index, not the final word).
2. If anything on the list is test-required — or you simply want the option — test in junior spring, retake in senior early fall.
3. Put registration deadlines on the family calendar, not just test dates: roughly two weeks ahead for the SAT, five weeks for the ACT.
4. Prep costs nothing if you don't want it to: Khan Academy for the SAT, plus the free official practice on [act.org](https://act.org).
5. Verify every date and policy at the source. Testing policies have flipped twice in six years; assume they can flip again.
