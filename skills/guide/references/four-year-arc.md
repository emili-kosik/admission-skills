# The Four-Year Arc: Grades 9-12 at Altitude

Two truths frame everything below. First: **nothing in 9th grade is fatal.** A rough freshman semester, a quit activity, a B-minus in biology — admissions readers see four-year trajectories, and an upward one is a good story. Second: **junior year matters most.** It is the last full year of grades on the transcript at application time, the year of the tests, the recommenders, and the list. Budget family energy accordingly: light early, focused in the middle, executional at the end.

| Grade | The one job | Intensity |
|---|---|---|
| 9th | Build rigor habits; explore activities | Deliberately light |
| 10th | Go deeper in fewer things; scout the tests | Moderate |
| 11th | The pivot year: tests, list, recommenders, essay | Highest |
| 12th fall | Execute applications | High but mechanical |
| 12th spring | Decide well | Emotional, not logistical |

## 9th grade: habits, not a résumé

- **Fall:** take the hardest course load the student can handle *with sleep intact*. Rigor habits — doing the reading, asking for help early — matter more than any single grade.
- **Activities:** join several, quit freely. This is the exploration year; depth comes later. No admissions office expects a 14-year-old to have a "spike."
- **Spring:** sketch the four-year course arc with the school counselor (e.g., when honors and AP courses become available, what the math sequence allows). Plan a low-key summer: a job, a project, a program, real rest — all fine.
- Nothing this year is fatal. Do not pay anyone who implies otherwise.

## 10th grade: depth over count

- **October:** most schools offer the PSAT 10 or a PSAT/NMSQT practice sitting — school-administered, no individual registration ([satsuite.collegeboard.org](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/psat-nmsqt/test-dates)). Treat it purely as a diagnostic; scores this year count for nothing.
- **All year:** trim the activity list and go deeper in the two or three that stuck. Leadership and sustained commitment beat a long list every time.
- **Spring:** take one free full-length practice SAT and one practice ACT at home, timed. Compare percentile results and how the student *felt* — that choice of test drives the whole junior-year calendar. Practice materials are free at [satsuite.collegeboard.org](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/sat/practice-preparation) and [act.org](https://www.act.org/content/act/en/products-and-services/the-act/test-preparation.html).
- **May:** AP exams (first two full weeks of May) if enrolled in AP courses.

## 11th grade: the pivot year

| When | What | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| October | **PSAT/NMSQT** | The junior-year sitting is the National Merit qualifier. Register through the school, not College Board ([satsuite.collegeboard.org](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/psat-nmsqt/test-dates)). |
| Winter | Begin test prep; draft an initial college list | Aim for a balanced reach/match/likely list. House rule: any college admitting under ~15% is a reach for everyone, regardless of stats. |
| Late Feb (typically) | Common App essay prompts announced | Prompts have changed little year to year; canonical page: [commonapp.org/apply/essay-prompts](https://www.commonapp.org/apply/essay-prompts). |
| Spring | **First SAT or ACT** | SAT typically offers Mar/May/Jun dates, ACT Feb/Apr/Jun — verify at [satsuite.collegeboard.org](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/sat/dates-deadlines) and [act.org](https://www.act.org/content/act/en/products-and-services/the-act/registration/test-dates.html). A first sitting now leaves fall for a retake. Check each college's test policy — required, optional, and test-free all exist ([fairtest.org](https://fairtest.org/test-optional-list/)). |
| Spring | **Ask two teachers for recommendations** | Junior-year teachers, asked before summer, are the norm. Ask in person; make it easy to say yes. |
| Spring | Meet the counselor; campus visits | Confirm senior-year rigor; visit campuses over spring break if practical (virtual tours count). |
| May | AP exams | First two full weeks of May. |
| Summer | **Draft the personal essay; finalize the list** | Prompts have been out since late winter. The essay is the student's voice — coaching, questions, and critique are fair; ghostwriting (by an adult or an AI) is defined as fraud under the [Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy). |

While building the list, run net-price calculators, not sticker prices. Sticker price is fiction; the net price for *your* income is the real number, and it varies enormously by college.

## 12th grade, fall: applications

- **August 1:** Common App opens for the new cycle ([commonapp.org](https://www.commonapp.org/)). Accounts created earlier roll over, including essay drafts.
- **Aug-Oct:** retake the SAT/ACT if needed — fall dates exist for both, but October sittings are typically the last comfortable ones for November deadlines.
- **October 1:** [FAFSA](https://studentaid.gov/h/apply-for-aid/fafsa) and [CSS Profile](https://cssprofile.collegeboard.org/) open. File within weeks — state and college priority deadlines vary widely and land much earlier than the federal one ([studentaid.gov deadline list](https://studentaid.gov/apply-for-aid/fafsa/fafsa-deadlines)).
- **October 1 - November 30:** the University of California filing window ([admission.universityofcalifornia.edu](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/)). UC uses its own application and essays (Personal Insight Questions), so California-bound students run a parallel track a month ahead of everyone else.
- **November (typically Nov 1 or 15):** Early Decision and Early Action deadlines at most colleges that offer them. **ED is binding** — apply ED only where the student would enroll sight-unseen at an affordable net price. EA is not binding.
- **December-January:** early decisions arrive (ED typically mid-December); Regular Decision deadlines cluster around Jan 1-15 at most colleges.
- Verify every deadline on each college's official admissions page. Aggregators and this guide are starting points, never the authority.

## 12th grade, spring: decisions

- **February-early April:** ED2 decisions (typically mid-February), then Regular Decision results (typically mid-March to early April).
- **Compare offers by net cost**, line by line — grants vs. loans vs. work-study. An "award" letter heavy on loans is not an award.
- **May 1:** the national candidate reply date — deposit at exactly one college. AP exams run the first two full weeks of May.
- **May-July:** waitlist movement (no guarantees — the deposited college is the plan), final transcript sent, and for those on a waitlist, keep the deposit down elsewhere until an offer is in writing.

## The international overlay

Everything above applies, plus three additions on top of the same calendar:

- **English proficiency (TOEFL/IELTS/Duolingo):** most colleges require one; test by early fall of senior year so scores arrive before November deadlines. Verify each college's accepted tests and minimums on its own page.
- **Credential evaluation:** some colleges require a course-by-course evaluation of non-US transcripts (others evaluate in-house). It typically takes weeks — order in early fall of application year if required.
- **After the deposit (spring):** the enrolled college issues the Form I-20, then SEVIS fee, DS-160, and the embassy interview. An F-1 visa can be issued up to 365 days before the program start, but entry to the US is allowed no more than 30 days before it ([travel.state.gov](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/study/student-visa.html)). Book the interview early; wait times vary widely by post.

## Starting late? The arc compresses fine

A family opening this guide in junior spring — or senior September — has skipped nothing irreplaceable. The 9th-10th grade material is about reducing stress, not gatekeeping outcomes. From wherever you are: next test date, recommender asks, list, essay, deadlines. In that order.
