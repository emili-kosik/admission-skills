# Money: What College Actually Costs

Sticker price is fiction. Net price is real. Every dollar decision in this process gets easier once the family agrees on a real annual budget — and that conversation belongs in **junior spring**, not April of senior year when the offers are already on the table.

## Sticker vs net price

- **Sticker price** (published cost of attendance) is what almost nobody pays at private colleges and what many families don't pay at publics either.
- **Net price** = sticker price minus grants and scholarships (money you don't repay). It is the only number worth comparing.
- Net price **by family income bracket** is published federal data for every college — look any school up on [College Scorecard](https://collegescorecard.ed.gov/). A college with a higher sticker price is often *cheaper* for your family than one with a lower sticker price. You cannot know until you check.

## Two kinds of aid

| | Need-based aid | Merit aid |
|---|---|---|
| Based on | Family finances (FAFSA / CSS Profile) | Grades, scores, talents — no financial forms required for eligibility at many schools |
| Where it's biggest | Wealthy private colleges with large endowments | Colleges a notch below the student's academic profile, trying to attract them |
| Rule of thumb | The most selective colleges typically give **only** need-based aid — no merit money at all | Merit flows where the student is above the school's usual admit profile |

Both can appear in the same offer. Verify each college's aid philosophy on its financial aid page.

## The forms

| Form | Opens | Who files it | Cost |
|---|---|---|---|
| [FAFSA](https://studentaid.gov/h/apply-for-aid/fafsa) | **Oct 1** of senior year | US citizens and eligible noncitizens; required for all federal aid and most state aid | Free — anyone charging you for FAFSA help is a red flag |
| [CSS Profile](https://cssprofile.collegeboard.org/) | **Oct 1** of senior year | Required by many private colleges (and used by many for international applicants) for their own institutional aid | Free for families below an income threshold (recently $100,000/year — verify current terms on the Profile site); a fee otherwise |
| [IDOC](https://cssprofile.collegeboard.org/idoc) | After Profile submission | Uploads tax returns/W-2s to colleges that request them — wait until College Board emails you; deadlines are midnight ET | Included |

Two things that trip families up:

1. **The FAFSA federal deadline is not your deadline.** State priority deadlines can fall as early as December through March, and college priority deadlines are often earlier still. Check your state on the [official FAFSA deadline list](https://studentaid.gov/apply-for-aid/fafsa/fafsa-deadlines) and verify each college's date on its own aid page. File in October; aid at many schools is first-come, first-served.
2. **The form-year naming is confusing.** You file the FAFSA labeled for the year you'll be *enrolled* — a senior applying in fall 2026 files the 2027–28 FAFSA.

## Net Price Calculators: run three in junior spring

Every US college is federally required to host a Net Price Calculator on its website. It takes 15–20 minutes with a recent tax return and returns an estimate of what *your family* would actually pay.

**Run at least three — a public flagship, a private with strong aid, and a likely admit — in junior spring, BEFORE building the list.** If a category of school is unaffordable, you want to know before anyone falls in love with it. This is also the natural moment for the budget conversation: the parent names the real number the family can pay per year, out loud, and the list gets built around it.

One house rule intersects here: **ED is binding.** You commit before you can compare offers, so never apply Early Decision to a college whose Net Price Calculator you haven't run. Colleges typically release ED admits whose aid genuinely makes attendance impossible, but that is a stressful escape hatch, not a plan.

## Need-blind vs need-aware

- **Need-blind**: the admissions office doesn't see whether you applied for aid.
- **Need-aware**: ability to pay can be a factor, usually at the margins.

For domestic applicants, many colleges are need-blind or close to it. For **international applicants, most US colleges are need-AWARE** — asking for significant aid can affect admission odds, and only a small number of colleges are both need-blind and full-need for internationals. This isn't a reason not to apply for aid (unfunded admission you can't pay for helps no one); it's a reason to check each college's stated policy for international aid and build a list with realistic financial matches. FAFSA is generally not available to international students — most colleges use the CSS Profile or their own form instead; verify per college.

## How to read an award letter

Award letters are marketing documents. Decode them:

| Line item | What it really is | Repay? |
|---|---|---|
| Grants / scholarships | Free money | No |
| Federal Direct loans (subsidized/unsubsidized) | Borrowed money — reasonable in moderation ([studentaid.gov](https://studentaid.gov/understand-aid/types/loans)) | Yes, by the student |
| Parent PLUS / private loans | Borrowed money at higher stakes — a *financing suggestion*, not aid | Yes, with interest |
| Work-study | A job the student may get; paid as wages during the term, not subtracted from the bill | It's earned |

The comparison number for every school: **cost of attendance − grants/scholarships = your real net price.** Some letters blur loans into "total aid" to make the bottom line look smaller — recompute it yourself for every offer. Also check whether grants are renewable all four years and what GPA or enrollment conditions they carry.

## Scholarship strategy

- **Local first.** Community foundations, employers, unions, civic clubs, your high school's own list — smaller dollar amounts but dramatically less competition than national contests.
- **Free federal search:** the Department of Labor's [CareerOneStop Scholarship Finder](https://www.careeronestop.org/Toolkit/Training/find-scholarships.aspx) indexes thousands of scholarships, fellowships, and grants at no cost.
- **Never pay a fee** to apply for a scholarship or to "unlock" a match. Fees are the signature of a scam.
- **Ask about displacement.** Many colleges reduce their own aid when outside scholarships arrive; ask each aid office how outside awards are treated (grants reduced first, or loans/work-study first — the second is much better for you).
- Scholarship essays recycle: the same coached-never-ghostwritten rule applies as for application essays.

## The junior-spring money checklist

1. Parent and student agree on a real annual budget number.
2. Run 3+ Net Price Calculators before the list exists.
3. Note which target colleges require the CSS Profile in addition to FAFSA.
4. Calendar Oct 1 (FAFSA + CSS Profile open) and your state's priority deadline.
5. Start the local scholarship list now — many local deadlines land in winter of senior year.

Every deadline above except the fixed Oct 1 openings varies by college and state: verify on the official page, every time.
