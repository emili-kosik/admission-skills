# Application Rounds and Deadlines

Colleges admit students through a handful of application "rounds," each with its own deadline, decision date, and rules about commitment. Picking rounds is a strategy decision, not a formality — especially Early Decision, which is a binding contract. This chapter covers the 2026–27 cycle (applying fall 2026 for fall 2027 enrollment).

**The one rule that overrides everything here: verify every deadline on each college's official admissions page.** The dates below are the typical pattern, and colleges deviate from it every year.

## The rounds at a glance

| Round | Binding? | Typical deadline | Typical decision | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Early Decision (ED) | **Yes** | Nov 1 (some Nov 15 or Dec 1) | Mid-December | If admitted, you enroll and withdraw all other applications |
| Early Action (EA) | No | November (often Nov 1) | Dec 1 – Jan 31, some to mid-Feb | Early answer, no commitment |
| Restrictive/Single-Choice EA (REA/SCEA) | No | Typically Nov 1 | Typically mid-December | Nonbinding, but limits where else you may apply early |
| Early Decision 2 (ED2) | **Yes** | Typically Jan 1–15 | Typically mid-February | Same binding contract as ED, later calendar |
| Regular Decision (RD) | No | Typically Jan 1–15 | Mid-March to early April | The default round at most colleges |
| Rolling | No | Window opens ~Aug–Sep, stays open until the class fills | Typically 4–8 weeks after you submit | Earlier is better — seats and aid deplete |

Source for the ED/EA pattern: [College Board BigFuture on early decision and early action](https://bigfuture.collegeboard.org/plan-for-college/apply-to-college/facts-about-early-decision-and-early-action).

## Early Decision: the family financial conversation comes first

ED is a signed agreement — the student, a parent or guardian, and the school counselor all sign it — that says: if admitted, you will enroll and withdraw every other application. That means:

- **You will see exactly one financial aid offer.** There is no April moment where you line up packages and negotiate. ED locks your aid comparison away.
- The generally accepted way out of an ED admission is a documented finding that the aid offer makes attendance unaffordable. That is a safety valve, not a plan — treat it as a last resort, and expect the college to scrutinize it.
- **Before anyone applies ED, the family must sit down and do the money conversation.** Run the college's net price calculator (every US college is required to publish one) with real family financials. Sticker price is fiction; net price is what you'll actually pay. If the calculator's number doesn't work, ED to that college doesn't work — full stop.
- File early: [FAFSA](https://studentaid.gov/apply-for-aid/fafsa/fafsa-deadlines) and the CSS Profile both open **October 1**, a month before most ED deadlines. ED applicants should submit aid forms alongside the application so the December decision arrives with an aid offer attached.
- **International applicants:** many colleges are need-aware for international students, and international aid budgets are smaller. If your family needs significant aid, ED narrows your options at exactly the moment you most need to compare offers. Think twice, and read each college's international aid policy before committing.

## REA / SCEA: nonbinding, but restrictive

A small group of highly selective colleges offer Restrictive Early Action (also called Single-Choice Early Action). You are **not** obligated to attend if admitted — but you agree not to apply early to other private colleges. Most versions still allow EA to public universities, rolling-admission schools, and non-US institutions, but **the exceptions differ school by school**. Read the exact policy page of the college in question before submitting anything else early; violating the restriction can void your application.

## Decision windows: when answers arrive

| What | Typical timing |
|---|---|
| ED decisions | Mid-December |
| EA decisions | December through January, some to mid-February |
| ED2 decisions | Around mid-February |
| RD decisions | Mid-March to early April |
| "Ivy Day" | The Ivy League has historically released RD decisions together in late March |
| **National reply date** | **May 1** — the deadline to accept one offer and deposit |

May 1 is the fixed anchor at the end of the funnel: colleges subscribing to the national candidate reply date give admitted students until May 1 to commit. Everything else in the table is a pattern — verify per college.

## Deferred or waitlisted: what each actually means

**Deferral** happens in the early rounds: instead of admitting or denying, the college moves your application into the Regular Decision pool for a second read. You are still alive. Typical moves: follow the college's deferral instructions exactly (some want a short letter of continued interest, some explicitly don't), make sure mid-year grades get sent, and add any genuinely new achievement. Then finish your RD applications as if the deferral were a denial — because statistically, at very selective colleges, most deferrals end that way.

**Waitlist** happens in the spring: the college may admit you if space opens after May 1. If you're interested, formally accept the waitlist spot — but **deposit at another college by May 1 anyway.** Waitlist movement typically runs May through July, is impossible to predict year to year, and late offers often come with limited financial aid and, for international students, a compressed visa timeline. Treat a waitlist as a lottery ticket, never a plan.

## Should you apply early? The honest trade-offs

Published early-round admit rates usually look higher than RD rates. That gap is real but heavily confounded:

- Early pools are self-selected and typically stronger — students who are organized enough to apply by Nov 1 with finished testing and essays.
- Recruited athletes, legacy applicants, and other institutional priorities are concentrated in the early round at many colleges, inflating the early number in ways that don't apply to an unhooked applicant.
- **A sub-15% admit rate is a reach for everyone, in every round.** No early plan changes that.

ED makes sense when all three are true:

1. The college is a clear, researched first choice — not just the most prestigious name on the list.
2. The family has run the net price calculator and the number works (the financial conversation above has already happened).
3. The application is genuinely ready by November — grades, testing, and essays don't need senior-fall improvement. Essays should be the student's own work, coached but never ghostwritten; the [Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy) treats submitted AI- or ghostwritten content as application fraud.

ED is the wrong tool when the family needs to compare aid offers, when senior-fall grades or a retest would strengthen the application, or when the "first choice" is really a coin flip among three schools. In those cases, EA gives you an early answer with no strings, and ED2 in January keeps a binding option open for a first choice that crystallizes after the early results land.

Rolling admission rewards speed: applying in September to a rolling school can put an acceptance in hand before your first reach decision even arrives — a cheap, powerful anti-panic move.

## Calendars that don't follow the pattern

- **University of California:** its own application, no ED/EA/RD rounds at all. The filing window is **October 1 – November 30**. Confirm the current window at [admission.universityofcalifornia.edu](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/).
- Several universities (MIT, Georgetown, and others) use their own portals or non-standard early plans — their round names and rules don't map one-to-one onto the table above.
- State universities and public flagships often have earlier priority deadlines for scholarships and honors programs than for plain admission.

None of these are exotic — they just mean the same thing everything in this chapter means: **build your deadline list from each college's official admissions page, not from a general calendar, and re-check it in the fall, because dates move.**
