# Decisions and After: March to Move-In

Decisions arrive, money gets compared, one deposit goes in, and senior year still has to be finished. This chapter covers the stretch from the first decision letter to the first day on campus — including the visa chain for international students, which starts the day you deposit.

## The four possible outcomes

| Outcome | What it means | What you do |
|---|---|---|
| **Admit** | You're in, usually with an aid offer attached (sometimes arriving separately) | Compare offers (below); reply by the deadline in the letter — for regular decision that is the national reply date, **May 1** |
| **Deny** | Final for this cycle at most colleges | Move on to your other offers. Appeals exist at some colleges but typically succeed only with significant *new* information (a major grade update, a clerical error) — check the college's official policy before writing one |
| **Waitlist** | Not an offer. You may be admitted later if space opens | Accept the waitlist spot only if you'd genuinely attend, and **deposit at a college that admitted you anyway** (see below) |
| **Defer** | Your early (ED/EA) application is moved into the regular pool for a spring decision | Send what the college invites — typically mid-year grades and a brief continued-interest note. If you were deferred from ED, the binding commitment typically dissolves; confirm in the deferral letter |

Timing patterns: ED decisions typically mid-December, EA December–January, ED2 mid-February, regular decision mid-March to early April. Verify each college's own announcement — never assume.

## Comparing offers line by line

Aid letters are not standardized, and they routinely blur the line between money you receive and money you borrow. Rebuild each offer in a single table:

| Line item | What to check |
|---|---|
| **Cost of attendance (COA)** | Tuition + fees + housing + food + books + travel. Colleges sometimes lowball travel and personal costs — use your own numbers |
| **Grants and scholarships** | Free money. This is the only line that reduces what you actually pay |
| **Loans** | **Loans are not aid.** They are your future money, repaid with interest ([studentaid.gov](https://studentaid.gov/understand-aid/types/loans)). Subtract them from the "aid" total before comparing |
| **Work-study** | Real but earned later, hour by hour — it doesn't reduce the bill due in August |
| **Net price** | COA minus grants/scholarships only. Sticker price is fiction; net price is real |
| **Renewal terms** | Is the scholarship guaranteed for 4 years? What GPA keeps it? Is year-1 grant money front-loaded? |

**Compare 4-year cost, not year-1 cost.** A generous freshman package with a hard GPA renewal condition and 4% annual tuition growth can end up costing more than a flatter offer. Multiply it out. Cross-check a college's typical net price by income bracket on [College Scorecard](https://collegescorecard.ed.gov/) or [College Navigator](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/).

## Aid reconsideration is a real process — use it

If your family's finances changed (job loss, medical costs, a divorce, a new sibling in college) or a comparable college offered meaningfully more, contact the financial aid office and ask about their **reconsideration or appeal process**. Most colleges have one; aid officers can exercise professional judgment on documented changed circumstances ([studentaid.gov](https://studentaid.gov/)). Be factual, attach documentation, and ask before May 1. This is a normal request, not a confrontation — the worst outcome is "no."

## Waitlist reality

- Movement, when it happens, is typically **May through July**, after colleges see who deposited.
- Odds vary wildly by college and by year — treat a waitlist spot as a lottery ticket, not a plan.
- **You must still deposit at an admitted college by May 1.** If the waitlist later comes through and you switch, you typically forfeit that first deposit — that's the expected, allowed path (sequential, not simultaneous).
- Before accepting a late waitlist offer, ask what aid comes with it. Aid budgets can be thinner by summer.

## May 1: deposit at exactly ONE college

May 1 is the national candidate reply date. Two rules:

1. **One deposit.** Submitting enrollment deposits at two or more colleges ("double-depositing") to keep options open violates the enrollment terms most colleges attach to their offers, and discovery can get offers withdrawn at both. The only legitimate second deposit is the sequential waitlist case above.
2. **Decline the rest.** Formally saying no to your other offers is courteous and it releases spots — including waitlist movement for someone else.

If a college's own deadline differs from May 1 (rolling admission, some publics), the letter governs. Verify on the college's official page.

## Finish strong: offers are conditional

Every admission offer is contingent on you finishing senior year at the level you were admitted with. Colleges receive a **final transcript** (typically sent by your school in June) and they read it. A slide from A's to B's is normal life; failed classes, dropped rigor, or disciplinary problems can draw a warning letter, revised terms, or a rescinded offer.

Practical list for admitted seniors:

- Keep grades at a recognizable version of your application self through the final semester.
- Take your AP exams (first two full weeks of May) — scores typically arrive in early July and can convert to credit or placement at your chosen college.
- Have your school send the final transcript to the one college you deposited at; send AP scores in July.
- Complete the college's post-deposit checklist: housing application, orientation registration, health forms, placement tests. These have their own deadlines — read the admitted-student portal weekly.

## International students: the F-1 chain starts the day you deposit

The visa sequence is strictly ordered and each step gates the next — start immediately after depositing (typically May):

| Step | What happens |
|---|---|
| 1. Request the **Form I-20** | Your college registers you in SEVIS and issues the I-20; you'll typically need to show proof of finances first — ask the international office what documents they require |
| 2. Pay the **SEVIS I-901 fee** | Required before the visa interview |
| 3. Complete the **DS-160** | The online visa application |
| 4. Book the **embassy/consulate interview** | Wait times vary widely by post — book as early as possible; an F-1 visa can be issued up to 365 days before your program start date ([travel.state.gov](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/study/student-visa.html)) |
| 5. Enter the US | **No more than 30 days before your program start date** — plan arrival for that window (typically late July–August) |

Bring the I-20, financial documents, and admission letter to the interview; keep the I-20 with your passport when you travel. Your college's international student office is the authoritative guide for steps 1 and 5 — use them.

## Bottom line

Read every decision letter's actual terms, compare offers on 4-year net price with loans stripped out, deposit at exactly one college by May 1, keep your grades intact, and — if you're international — start the I-20 chain the same week you deposit. Verify every date and policy on the college's official page.
