# Orientation for International Families

US admissions is not a harder version of your home system — it is a different game with different rules. Nothing here is unlearnable; most of it is unfamiliar. This chapter names the parts that have no equivalent in most national systems, then gives you the vocabulary. Cycle basis: applying in fall 2026 for fall 2027 enrollment.

## Seven things that likely do not exist back home

| Concept | What it actually means | What to do |
|---|---|---|
| **Holistic review** | No single exam decides. Grades and course rigor, essays, recommendations, and activities are read together as one file. High scores guarantee nothing: any college with a sub-15% admit rate is a reach for everyone. | Build a balanced list; do not anchor on one "dream school." |
| **Personal essays** | Colleges ask about your life, values, and setbacks — not your academic mastery. This feels intrusive in many cultures; it is expected here. | Essays are coached, never ghostwritten — AI- or parent-written essays are application fraud under the [Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy). |
| **Recommendation culture** | Teachers and the counselor write candid, private letters. Students typically waive the right to read them — that waiver makes the letters credible. | Ask two junior-year teachers early; give them context, never a script. |
| **Demonstrated interest** | Some colleges track opened emails, visits, and optional interviews as an admission factor. Others ignore it entirely. | A college's Common Data Set (item C7) shows whether "level of applicant's interest" is considered. |
| **Need-aware aid for internationals** | At most colleges, an international applicant's request for financial aid can affect the admission decision, even where domestic review is need-blind. Only a small number of colleges say they are need-blind for internationals. | Read each college's international aid page before deciding whether and where to apply for aid. |
| **Credential evaluation** | Many colleges require a course-by-course evaluation of non-US transcripts through a service such as WES or ECE; others evaluate in-house. Turnaround is typically 4–8 weeks. | Check each college's policy in early fall and order evaluations immediately if required. |
| **The school report** | The Common App expects your school — not you — to submit a school report, official transcript, and (ideally) a school profile explaining its grading system. Many schools outside the US have never done this. | Brief your counselor or head of school in September; walk them through the Common App recommender system yourself if needed. |

## English proficiency tests

Most colleges require one of these unless you qualify for a waiver (commonly several years of English-medium schooling — policies vary widely; verify on each college's international admissions page).

| Test | Typical score turnaround | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| TOEFL iBT | ~3–4 days to your account | Most widely accepted |
| IELTS Academic | ~1–5 days computer, ~13 days paper | Check that the college accepts IELTS |
| Duolingo English Test | ~2 days | Cheapest and fastest; acceptance is broad but not universal — verify per college |

**The timing rule:** colleges need your scores delivered by the deadline, not just taken. Test by **September–October for November 1 ED/EA deadlines**, and by **October–November for January Regular Decision deadlines**. Leave room for one retake.

## Financial documentation

- Most colleges ask international applicants for a **certification (or declaration) of finances** plus **recent bank statements** showing funds for at least the first year. Do this paperwork once, carefully: the same documents are what the college later uses to issue your I-20.
- The **FAFSA** is only for US citizens and eligible noncitizens ([studentaid.gov](https://studentaid.gov/)). International applicants seeking aid typically file the **CSS Profile** (opens Oct 1) or the college's own international aid form — check which one each college wants.
- The house rule still applies to you: **sticker price is fiction, net price is real** — but international aid budgets are smaller and usually need-aware, so run each college's Net Price Calculator knowing it may not model international aid. Verify on the college's international aid page.

## After the acceptance: the F-1 chain

Every link depends on the previous one, so start the day you deposit (by the **May 1** national reply date):

1. **Deposit and request your I-20.** The college verifies your financial documents, registers you in **SEVIS**, and issues **Form I-20**.
2. **Pay the SEVIS I-901 fee** once you have the I-20.
3. **File the DS-160** online visa application.
4. **Book the embassy or consulate interview** — immediately. Wait times vary enormously by city; this is the step that strands people.
5. **Interview and visa issuance.** An F-1 visa can be issued up to 365 days before your program start date, but you may not enter the US more than 30 days before it. Source: [travel.state.gov](https://travel.state.gov/content/travel/en/us-visas/study/student-visa.html).
6. **Arrive within the 30-day window** (typically late July–August) with your I-20 and financial documents in hand luggage.

## Systems outside the Common App

Not every college uses the Common App. The University of California is the big one: one application for nine undergraduate campuses, a firm **October 1 – November 30** filing window, no recommendation letters for most applicants, test-free, and eight short Personal Insight Questions (you answer any four, 350 words each) instead of a personal essay ([admission.universityofcalifornia.edu](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/)). MIT, Georgetown, and Texas public universities also run their own portals. Your tracker should record which system each college uses.

## Glossary

| Term | Plain meaning |
|---|---|
| **ED** | Early Decision — apply early (typically Nov 1), decision around mid-December. **Binding**: if admitted, you withdraw all other applications. You can apply ED to only one college. |
| **EA** | Early Action — apply early, hear early, **not** binding. |
| **REA** | Restrictive (or Single-Choice) Early Action — nonbinding, but limits where else you may apply early. Rules differ per college; read them exactly. |
| **RD** | Regular Decision — deadlines typically cluster Jan 1–15; decisions mid-March to early April. |
| **GPA** | Grade point average, usually on a 4.0 scale. Your school's grades get converted or evaluated — you do not convert them yourself. |
| **Weighted / unweighted GPA** | Unweighted caps at 4.0; weighted adds bonus points for honors/AP/IB courses. Colleges often recalculate their own way. |
| **AP** | Advanced Placement — US college-level courses with national exams in the first two full weeks of May. |
| **IB** | International Baccalaureate — widely recognized; colleges use predicted grades at application time. |
| **PSAT** | Practice SAT, administered by schools each October (grades 10–11). |
| **NMSQT** | The 11th-grade PSAT sitting doubles as the National Merit Scholarship qualifier (US citizens/residents). |
| **Common App** | Shared online application accepted by roughly 1,100 colleges; opens/refreshes every **August 1** ([commonapp.org](https://www.commonapp.org/)). |
| **CDS** | Common Data Set — standardized statistics each college publishes yearly; section C covers admissions factors and deadlines. |
| **FAFSA** | Free Application for Federal Student Aid; opens **October 1**; US citizens and eligible noncitizens only ([studentaid.gov](https://studentaid.gov/)). |
| **SAI** | Student Aid Index — the number the FAFSA produces to measure ability to pay (replaced the old "EFC"). |
| **CSS Profile** | College Board's aid form used by many private colleges; opens **October 1**; the main aid form for most international applicants. |
| **NPC** | Net Price Calculator — every college must host one; estimates what you would actually pay. May not model international aid. |
| **Need-blind** | Ability to pay is not considered in the admission decision. |
| **Need-aware** | Ability to pay can influence the decision — the norm for international applicants. |
| **Demonstrated interest** | Trackable signals (visits, emails, optional interviews) that you would enroll; considered by some colleges, ignored by others. |
| **Waitlist** | Neither admitted nor denied; offers, if any, typically come May–July. Treat as a no and secure a seat elsewhere by May 1. |
| **Deferral** | An early (ED/EA) application pushed into the Regular Decision pool for a second read — not a rejection. |
| **I-20** | The form your college issues certifying enrollment and proven funding; the foundation of the visa process. |
| **SEVIS** | The US government database tracking international students; registration triggers the I-901 fee. |
| **DS-160** | The online nonimmigrant visa application form. |
| **F-1** | The student visa status most undergraduates hold. |

One rule above all: **verify every deadline and policy on each college's official admissions page.** Grids and guides — including this one — describe patterns; the college's own page is the contract.
