# Recommendations and School Reports

Letters of recommendation are the part of the application someone else writes — which means your job is logistics, not prose. Ask the right people, ask early, give them good raw material, and track the paperwork. This chapter covers who writes what, when to ask, the FERPA waiver, mid-year reports, and how to do all of it graciously. Cycle basis: applying in fall 2026 for fall 2027 entry.

## Who writes what

| Document | Written by | What it covers |
|---|---|---|
| Teacher evaluation (typically 2) | Academic-subject teachers, usually from 11th grade | How the student thinks and works in a classroom |
| Counselor recommendation | School counselor | The student in the context of the whole school |
| School report | School counselor (a form, not a letter) | Transcript, GPA, curriculum rigor, school profile |
| Optional/other recommender | Coach, employer, clergy, research mentor | Only where a college explicitly allows it |
| Mid-year report | School counselor | First-semester senior grades, sent after applications |

The counselor letter and school report are the school's responsibility, but the student initiates them — by adding the counselor as a recommender inside the application platform and by keeping the counselor informed of the college list.

## Whom to ask, and when

- **Ask two teachers in the SPRING of junior year.** This is the single most time-sensitive move in this chapter. Teachers who write well get booked; asking in May of 11th grade beats asking in September of 12th, when the same teacher may have twenty requests and hazier memories.
- **Junior-year teachers in core academic subjects** (math, science, English, history, world language) are the norm. They taught the student recently, at the most advanced level colleges will see completed.
- **Pick teachers who know the student, not just the grade.** A B+ in a class with genuine engagement usually produces a stronger letter than an A earned silently in the back row.
- Many colleges suggest or require teachers from two different subject areas (e.g., one STEM, one humanities) — check each college's requirements rather than assuming.
- If a college allows an extra recommender, use it only when that person adds something the teachers cannot (a research mentor, a long-term employer). More letters are not better letters.

## How many letters each college actually wants

Counts vary: some colleges require two teacher letters, some one, some none. Two anchors:

- The [Common App Requirements Grid](https://appsupport.commonapp.org/applicantsupport/s/article/Where-can-I-find-the-Requirements-Grid) is a free, public PDF listing deadlines, fees, and recommendation requirements for all Common App member colleges, updated during the cycle. Use it to build your tracking table — then **verify each college's count on its own admissions page**, because the grid can lag mid-cycle changes.
- **The University of California does not accept letters of recommendation for most freshman applicants.** No teacher letters, no counselor letter — a handful of campuses/programs may invite letters later, after review, but you do not send them up front. See [UC's application instructions](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/how-to-apply/). If your list is UC-heavy, the letter workload drops accordingly; if it mixes UC and Common App schools, the letters are only for the latter.

## Brag sheets: the raw material

Most high schools ask the student (and often a parent) to fill out a "brag sheet" — a questionnaire the counselor and teachers use to write specific, detailed letters. Take it seriously; a vague brag sheet produces a vague letter.

A useful student brag sheet, even if the school doesn't require one:

- Three specific classroom moments: a project, a question you chased, a time you struggled and recovered
- Activities and roles, with what you actually did (not just titles)
- What you plan to study and why, in two or three sentences
- Anything the recommender might not know: work hours, family responsibilities, obstacles you're comfortable sharing

The parent brag sheet is the one place in this process where a parent is *supposed* to write about the kid — anecdotes and context, not adjectives. Note the boundary: brag sheets inform the letter; the recommender writes it. Students never draft their own recommendation letters, just as essays are coached, never ghostwritten ([Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy)).

## The FERPA waiver: check "waive"

Inside the Common App, before inviting recommenders, students complete a FERPA release authorization — a choice to waive or retain the right to view recommendation letters after enrolling (FERPA is the federal law giving enrolled students access to their education records; see [Common App](https://www.commonapp.org/) for the exact wording in the current application).

**Waiving is the norm, and here's why:** a confidential letter carries more weight. Admissions readers know a recommender writes more candidly when the student will never see the letter, and many teachers and counselors simply decline to write non-confidential letters. Retaining access can quietly signal distrust and weaken the very letters you worked to secure. The waiver decision is typically permanent once recommenders are invited, so make it deliberately — and make it "waive" unless you have an unusual, specific reason.

This is not a loss of control. Control comes earlier: choosing recommenders who know and like the student.

## Mid-year reports

Most colleges ask for a **mid-year report** — first-semester (or first-trimester) senior grades plus any updates — sent by the counselor after the application, typically in January or February. Two things to know:

- **The student usually doesn't send it, but should confirm it went.** Ask the counselor in January; check each college's applicant portal for a received checkmark.
- **Senior grades still count.** Mid-year reports are where senioritis becomes visible, and offers of admission — including binding ED offers (ED is binding) — are contingent on maintained performance. A final report with the full transcript follows after graduation.

## Notes for international applicants

- Teachers outside the US may be unfamiliar with the anecdotal, evaluative style US colleges expect. Share a sample structure (how the student thinks, specific moments, comparison to peers) and generous lead time.
- Letters typically must be in English or accompanied by a certified translation — verify each college's policy on its international admissions page.
- If your school has no counselor, Common App allows a principal, head teacher, or other school official to fill that role and submit the school report — see [commonapp.org](https://www.commonapp.org/) support resources.

## How to ask well — and how to say thank you

**The ask (in person or live video beats email):** "I really enjoyed your class, and I'm applying to college this fall. Would you feel comfortable writing me a strong letter of recommendation?" The word *strong* gives a lukewarm writer a graceful exit — which is a gift to you.

Then, within a day or two, follow up in writing with:

1. The brag sheet or a short summary of relevant moments from their class
2. Your college list with the **earliest** deadline flagged (verify every deadline on each college's official page)
3. Exactly how they'll receive the request (e.g., "I'll invite you through Common App by September 1")

**Afterward:** a handwritten thank-you note beats email; send it once letters are submitted, not months later. And in spring, tell them where you got in and where you're going — for most teachers, that news is the actual payment. Writing letters is unpaid labor done on evenings and weekends; treat it that way from the first ask to the last update.
