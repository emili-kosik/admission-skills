# How Admissions Actually Works: Holistic Review

Most US colleges do not admit by formula. There is no national entrance exam, no cutoff score, no ranked list where the top N applicants get in.

Selective colleges practice **holistic review**: a human being reads the whole file — transcript, essays, activities, recommendations, and the context you come from — and the admissions office assembles a *class*, not a leaderboard. Understanding this one idea removes most of the panic from the process.

## The three questions behind every read

Strip away the jargon and a holistic reader is answering three things:

1. **Can this student do the work here?** — answered mostly by rigor and grades, in the context of what their school offered.
2. **What will they add to the community?** — answered by essays, activities, and recommendations.
3. **What does their context tell us?** — answered by the school profile, family circumstances, and geography.

Everything in the file is evidence for one of those three. Keep them in mind and the rest of this chapter follows naturally.

## What colleges weigh

Every college states its own weights, and you can look them up. Each school publishes a **Common Data Set** (CDS), and Section C7 lists exactly which factors it considers "very important," "important," "considered," or "not considered." Search `"<college name> common data set"` — the standard definitions live at [commondataset.org](https://commondataset.org/).

The table below is the typical pattern at selective colleges; always check the C7 of the schools on your list.

| Factor | What readers actually look at | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Rigor of curriculum** | Did you take the hardest courses reasonably available *at your school*? (honors, AP/IB, dual enrollment — or your country's equivalent) | Consistently at or near the top of the list. An A in a challenging course beats an A+ in an easy one. |
| **Grades (GPA)** | The trend matters as much as the number — an upward junior-year arc reads well | Read together with rigor, never in isolation. |
| **Essays** | Your voice, your judgment, evidence you'd contribute to a community | The one part of the file fully in your control senior fall. Coaching is fine; ghostwriting (human or AI) is application fraud under the [Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy). |
| **Activities** | Depth, commitment, and impact over time — including jobs and family responsibilities | A part-time job or caring for siblings *is* an activity. Depth beats a long list. |
| **Recommendations** | Teachers and counselor confirming who you are in a classroom | Required counts vary by college and system — the University of California, for example, doesn't require them for most applicants. |
| **Test scores** | Where required or submitted — see the next section | Policy varies college by college; verify every school. |
| **Context** | Your school's offerings, family circumstances, geography, first-gen status, national education system | You are compared against your opportunities, not someone else's. |

That last row matters enormously, for domestic and international applicants alike. Readers see a profile of your high school, or receive your national credentials (typically with a course-by-course evaluation for non-US transcripts).

If your school offers three AP courses, nobody expects twelve. If your country has no honors track, that is the context you're read in.

## There is no single entrance exam

Unlike many national systems, the US has no unified admission test. The [SAT](https://satsuite.collegeboard.org/sat) and [ACT](https://www.act.org/) exist, but whether they matter depends entirely on the college. Policies split three ways in the 2026–27 cycle:

| Policy | Meaning | Where it stands (verify per college) |
|---|---|---|
| **Test-required** | Must submit SAT or ACT | A group of highly selective colleges (including most Ivies, MIT, Stanford, Georgetown) and some state systems have reinstated requirements |
| **Test-optional** | You choose whether scores are seen | Over 2,000 institutions — searchable at [fairtest.org](https://fairtest.org/test-optional-list/) |
| **Test-free** | Scores not considered at all | Includes the [University of California](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/) and CSU systems |

Policies have shifted repeatedly since 2020, so treat any list — including this one — as a starting point and confirm on each college's own admissions page before building a testing plan.

Even the essays aren't standardized. Most Common App colleges read one personal essay plus their own supplements.

The UC application instead asks applicants to answer 4 of its 8 short Personal Insight Questions ([admission.universityofcalifornia.edu](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/how-to-apply/)) — a different writing task entirely. The application *systems* on your list shape the work ahead.

## A class is assembled, not a score-ladder

Admissions offices are not filling seats from a single ranked queue. They are building a freshman class with many simultaneous goals: students for every department (someone has to major in classics), geographic and demographic breadth, athletes, musicians, future engineers, and — at many institutions — enrollment and budget realities.

These **institutional priorities** shift year to year and are invisible from the outside. A typical file's path at a selective college looks like this:

1. **A regional reader goes first.** At many selective colleges, your file is initially read by the admissions officer responsible for your region or country — someone who knows your school system and reads you against local context.
2. **Committee discussion.** Files the reader supports move to a committee, where that reader often acts as your advocate.
3. **Class shaping.** Late in the cycle, the dean balances the admitted class against institutional priorities — which is where equally strong applicants can receive different decisions.

Two practical consequences:

- You are not competing head-to-head with every applicant on Earth; you are being understood by someone whose job is to know your context.
- **Early Decision is one such institutional priority.** Colleges value applicants who commit, which is why ED admit rates often look higher. But remember the house rule: **ED is binding** — one ED school, and if admitted you withdraw all other applications. Never treat it as a cost-free odds boost.

## Why a 4.0 gets denied — and why that's survivable

At a college admitting fewer than 1 in 20 applicants (the most selective run under 4%, per federal [College Scorecard](https://collegescorecard.ed.gov/) data), the arithmetic is unforgiving: there are far more academically flawless applicants than seats.

Admissions deans say this openly every cycle — most denied applicants were fully qualified. The decision was about the class being assembled, not about your student's worth or competence.

This is why the house rule exists: **any college admitting under ~15% of applicants is a reach for every applicant**, regardless of stats. A perfect GPA earns consideration, never a guarantee.

And it is survivable by design:

- You apply to a **balanced list** (likelies, matches, reaches), so no single decision decides the future.
- Holistic review runs both directions — a denial was a judgment about fit for *that* class; an admit chose *you*, not just your numbers.
- Every US applicant holds their offers until the national reply date of **May 1**, so decisions are compared side by side, never taken under pressure one at a time.
- The system's redundancy is the feature: with thousands of accredited four-year institutions ([nces.ed.gov](https://nces.ed.gov/collegenavigator/)), the question is never *whether* a great option exists — only which ones made it onto your list.

## What this means for your family

- **Control the controllables.** Course rigor and grades are years-long signals; essays and the college list are the senior-fall levers. Test scores are one factor at some schools, not the whole game.
- **Choose rigor your student can sustain.** The strongest transcript is the hardest load that still produces good grades and a functioning teenager — not the maximum theoretical load.
- **Read the C7 for every school on the list.** Ten minutes with a college's Common Data Set tells you more about what it values than any forum thread.
- **Essays are coached, never ghostwritten.** Brainstorming, questions, and critique are legitimate; someone else's prose — human or AI — is fraud under the [Common App policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy) and grounds for revoked admission.
- **Ignore sticker prices for now.** List price is fiction for most families; the *net price* after aid is what's real. Later chapters cover how to estimate it before applying.
- **Don't read a denial as a verdict.** It's a statement about one class at one institution in one year. A balanced list makes any single denial a non-event.
- **International families: the same logic applies.** You're read in the context of your national system, usually by a reader who knows it. Budget extra time for English proficiency tests and credential evaluation, and check each college's international requirements on its official pages.
- **Verify everything at the source.** Deadlines, test policies, and requirements described here are patterns; the college's own admissions page is the only authority.
