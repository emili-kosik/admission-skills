# Essays: The Part of the Application Only You Can Write

Grades and scores tell colleges what you did. Essays tell them who did it. This chapter covers the three essay genres you will actually face — the Common App personal essay, college-specific supplements, and the UC Personal Insight Questions — plus the rules about AI that now sit on top of all of them.

## The AI rules, before anything else

Read this section first, because it governs everything below. The [Common App fraud policy](https://www.commonapp.org/fraud-policy) defines submitting "the substantive content or output of an artificial intelligence platform" as **application fraud** — the same category as a plagiarized essay or a forged transcript. You sign an attestation that the work is yours. The same policy explicitly says that using AI to review grammar or spelling does **not** constitute fraud. Individual colleges then layer their own rules on top:

| College (examples) | Published stance |
|---|---|
| [Brown](https://admission.brown.edu/apply/how-apply/integrity-application-process) | Strictest: AI "not permitted under any circumstances" for application content, except basic proofreading-style grammar/spelling review |
| [Caltech](https://www.admissions.caltech.edu/apply/first-year-applicants/supplemental-application-essays/ethical-use-of-ai-guidelines-for-fall-applicants) | No AI-generated content to **outline or draft** an essay, no AI translation; AI-generated brainstorming *questions* and process research are fine |
| Yale | Substantive AI output = fraud; grammar review and topic suggestions at the start of the process are acceptable |
| Georgia Tech | Most permissive published policy: brainstorm, edit, refine — but the submission must be your own; never paste in content you did not create |
| [University of California](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/) | Runs plagiarism/AI checks on PIQs; unattributed AI content can disqualify you from UC admission entirely |

Policies evolve — verify each target college's current policy on its official admissions site before you rely on it.

**What this plugin will and won't do.** This plugin behaves to the strictest common standard (Brown/Caltech) by default. It will brainstorm with you using questions, critique your drafts, flag grammar and spelling, and help you research colleges and logistics. It will **never write, outline, or rewrite essay prose for you**, and it will refuse "make this sound more human" requests. This is not just compliance — it produces better essays. Admissions readers see thousands of essays a season; generic AI prose has a recognizable flatness, while the details only you know (the smell of your grandmother's kitchen, the exact wrong answer you gave in round two) are the whole competitive advantage. Coached, never ghostwritten.

## What essays are for

An application is mostly numbers and lists: GPA, course rigor, test scores, ten activity slots. Essays are the only channel that carries what the numbers cannot:

- **Voice.** How you think and talk when you are being yourself. Two applicants with identical stats are not identical people; the essay is where a reader learns which one you are.
- **Evidence of reflection.** Not what happened to you, but what you made of it. Selective colleges say this openly: they admit people, not resumes.
- **Context.** Family responsibilities, an unusual school, a move between countries — things a transcript cannot explain. (International applicants: this is also where you show comfort writing in English at length; that signal matters alongside any proficiency test.)

A useful mental model: the essay is not a trophy case. Your activities list already holds the trophies. The essay is a window.

## The Common App personal essay

One essay, **250–650 words**, sent to every Common App college you apply to. The seven prompts are announced in late February (typically — the 2026–27 set was announced in February 2026, unchanged from the prior year) and the canonical list lives at [commonapp.org/apply/essay-prompts](https://www.commonapp.org/apply/essay-prompts). The application itself opens **August 1** each year, and a rolled-over account preserves your draft.

| # | Prompt (paraphrased) |
|---|---|
| 1 | A background, identity, interest, or talent so meaningful your application would be incomplete without it |
| 2 | A challenge, setback, or failure — and what you learned |
| 3 | A time you questioned or challenged a belief or idea |
| 4 | Something someone did for you that sparked gratitude in a surprising way |
| 5 | An accomplishment, event, or realization that sparked growth and new self-understanding |
| 6 | A topic or idea so engaging you lose track of time |
| 7 | Any topic of your choice |

Practical notes:

- **The prompt matters less than people think.** Prompt 7 is "anything," and most strong essays fit several prompts. Find the story first, pick the prompt after.
- **Sensible sequence:** brainstorm in spring of junior year (prompts are already out), draft over the summer, revise in early fall. A summer draft plus two real revision passes beats an October scramble every time.
- There is also an optional "Challenges and Circumstances" question in the Writing section for describing disruptions or hardships — verify its exact form in the live application.

## Supplemental essays

Most selective colleges add their own essays on top of the personal essay — typically one to four per college, often 100–400 words each (lengths vary; check each college's application). These are where application workload actually lives: a 12-college list can mean 25+ supplements. The recurring archetypes:

| Archetype | What it is really asking | The trap |
|---|---|---|
| "Why us?" | Have you done real homework on *this* college — specific programs, professors, courses, communities? | The swap test: if you could replace the college's name with another and the essay still works, it says nothing. Rankings and campus beauty are not reasons. |
| Community / identity | What perspective do you carry, and what do you do with it? | Describing a community without showing your role in it. |
| Challenge / disagreement | How do you handle friction, failure, or ideas you dislike? | All storm, no aftermath — the reflection is the point. |
| Intellectual curiosity | What do you genuinely chase when nobody assigns it? | Faking passion for the major you think they want. |
| Activity elaboration | Depth on one commitment beyond the 150-character activities entry | Re-listing instead of zooming in on one moment. |

Reuse is legitimate — a community essay can often be adapted across colleges — but every "why us" must be written fresh. Build a spreadsheet of every supplement and its word count as soon as your list is final; supplements generally appear when applications open on or after August 1, but confirm each college's own pages.

## UC Personal Insight Questions: a different genre

The University of California does not use the Common App and does not want a personal essay. Its application asks **8 Personal Insight Questions — you answer any 4, at 350 words each** ([official PIQ guide](https://admission.universityofcalifornia.edu/how-to-apply/applying-as-a-freshman/personal-insight-questions.html)). The UC filing window is **October 1 – November 30**, a full month before most Regular Decision deadlines, so California-bound applicants need these done early.

Treat PIQs as **answers, not essays**. UC's own guidance is blunt about this: no hook needed, no narrative arc required, no metaphor budget. Readers want direct, specific, first-person responses to the question asked — think structured interview in writing. Four tight answers covering four *different* sides of you (leadership, creativity, a challenge, an academic passion, community work) beat four variations on one theme. And remember from the table above: UC checks PIQs for plagiarism and AI content, with disqualification on the line.

## What makes any of them work

Three qualities separate essays that land from essays that blur together:

1. **Specificity.** Concrete nouns, real scenes, actual dialogue. "I learned the value of hard work" is invisible; the 5:40 a.m. bus to the pool with wet hair freezing on the walk is not. If a sentence could appear in a thousand other applications, cut it.
2. **Voice.** The essay should sound like you on your most articulate day — not like a 45-year-old consultant, and not like a language model. Read it aloud; anywhere you stumble or cringe, that's not your voice.
3. **Reflection.** The event is the cheapest part of the essay. What you understood afterward — and how it changed what you do — is what readers are scoring. A modest story deeply examined outperforms a dramatic story merely reported.

The workflow this plugin supports: you talk, it asks questions; you draft, it critiques structure, specificity, and clarity; you revise, it flags grammar. Every word that gets submitted is yours — which is both the rule and the reason it works.
