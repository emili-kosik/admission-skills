# Interview question bank

How to use this file: these are the questions college interviewers actually
ask, grouped by what they are probing underneath. Do **not** hand the student
scripted answers. Instead, check that each group below has at least one of the
student's story anchors (see Step 2 of the skill) that can carry it, and let
the student practice out loud — ideally via the `mock-interviewer` agent.

Interviewers are rarely trying to trick anyone. Almost every question reduces
to three underlying reads: *Is this person curious? Can they talk about their
own life with specifics? Would they thrive here?*

## Group 1 — Background and self-presentation

The opener set. Alumni interviewers typically have not seen the application,
so the student is introducing themselves from zero.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "Tell me about yourself." | Can the student pick 2–3 defining things and stop, rather than reciting a résumé chronologically? The first 60 seconds set the tone. |
| "How would your friends describe you?" | Self-awareness, and whether the self-portrait matches how the student comes across in the room. |
| "Tell me about your school and your place in it." | Context: what was available to the student, and what they did with it. Also surfaces community role without asking directly. |
| "What do you do for fun, outside of school and activities?" | Whether there is a person behind the transcript. Genuine, small answers (cooking, pickup basketball, a game) land better than strategic ones. |
| "Who has influenced you most?" | Capacity for gratitude and reflection. The probe is the *why*, not the *who*. |
| "What are you most proud of?" | Values: what the student counts as an achievement, and whether they can own it without either bragging or deflecting. |

## Group 2 — Why us / fit

The only group that requires homework. Two or three specific, true reasons —
a program, a professor's work, a curriculum feature — beat any amount of
enthusiasm about prestige.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "Why do you want to attend this college?" | Whether the student did real research or is reciting rankings. Specifics only; "it's a great school" is a non-answer. |
| "What do you want to study, and why here specifically?" | Whether the academic interest connects to something this college actually offers. |
| "What will you contribute to campus?" | Whether the student sees college as a community they join, not a service they buy. One concrete continuation ("I'd keep doing X") suffices. |
| "What other colleges are you considering?" | Coherence of the list, not loyalty. Honest, brief, no rankings talk; it is fine to name a couple of peers and pivot back to fit. |
| "What are you looking for in a college?" | Whether the student has criteria at all, and whether this college plausibly meets them. |
| "Where do you see yourself in ten years?" | Direction, not a contract. "I don't know exactly, but..." followed by a real interest is a strong answer. |

## Group 3 — Academic and intellectual

The curiosity read. Interviewers at evaluative interviews typically weight
this group heavily.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "What's your favorite class, and why?" | Whether the *why* is about ideas ("we argued about X for a week") or about grades and ease. |
| "Tell me about a class or subject that challenged you." | Response to difficulty: resilience and study habits, told as a story rather than a complaint. |
| "What's an idea or problem that excites you right now?" | Genuine intellectual engagement — the single best predictor interviewers look for. Any field counts. |
| "What have you read, watched, or listened to lately that stuck with you?" | Curiosity outside assignments. A podcast or long video essay counts; pretending to love a classic backfires. |
| "Tell me about a teacher who shaped how you think." | Whether the student engages with mentors as people, and what kind of teaching they respond to. |
| "How do you learn best?" | Self-knowledge, and indirectly fit: seminar-lover vs lecture-hall-anonymous matters to some interviewers. |

## Group 4 — Activities and character

The depth read: interviewers typically prefer one activity explored for five
minutes over ten listed in one breath.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "Which activity means the most to you, and why?" | Depth and ownership. The follow-ups ("what exactly did you do?") are where thin involvement shows. |
| "Tell me about a time you led something." | Working definition of leadership — initiative and responsibility, with or without a title. |
| "Tell me about a failure or setback and what you did next." | Honesty and recovery. A real failure with a real lesson beats a humblebrag ("I care too much"). |
| "Walk me through a typical week." | How the pieces fit: workload, balance, and what the student chooses when time is scarce. |
| "Tell me about working with someone you disagreed with." | Maturity in conflict: can they describe the other side fairly? |
| "Which of your activities would you continue in college?" | Whether involvement is genuine (continues anywhere) or instrumental (ends at the application). |

## Group 5 — Curveballs and closers

Low-stakes improvisation checks. There is no right answer; the probe is
whether the student can think aloud pleasantly instead of freezing or
reciting.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "Tell me something about you that isn't in your application." | A gift, not a trap: one prepared-but-genuine small thing (a hobby, an obsession, a family role) works perfectly. |
| "If you had a completely free year, what would you do with it?" | What the student wants when nothing is required — curiosity and initiative unmasked. |
| "What would you change about your high school?" | Critical thinking without bitterness: can they critique an institution constructively? |
| "What's a news story or issue you've been following?" | Awareness of the world beyond school. Any sincere, moderately informed answer works; hot-take performance does not. |
| "Describe yourself in three words." | Composure and self-awareness under a slightly silly constraint. Words with a one-line story behind them beat adjectives. |
| "What should I have asked you that I didn't?" | The classic closer: hands the student the microphone. Have one anchor in reserve for exactly this moment. |

## Questions that are out of bounds

Interviewers should not ask about disabilities, religion, immigration status,
family finances, or other protected topics, and students never have to answer
chancing questions ("what were your scores?" is common but optional to
answer in informational interviews — a comfortable deflection is "I'd rather
tell you about..."). If an interviewer behaves inappropriately, the student
should end politely and the family should report it to the admissions office.

## The F-1 visa interview (international students)

A different interview entirely: 2–5 minutes with a consular officer, not
holistic, no essays. Under US law (INA §214(b)) every applicant is presumed
an intending immigrant until they convince the officer otherwise — so the
whole interview reduces to three screens: **ties to home country, funding,
and credible program choice**. Answers must be short, truthful, and
consistent with the I-20 and DS-160. Primary source: the State Department
student-visa page (`data/sources.json` → `international.student_visa`).
For the full process — I-20, SEVIS fee, DS-160, appointment timing — use the
`international` skill.

| Question | What it's actually probing |
|---|---|
| "Why this university?" | Genuine, specific program choice — same homework as "why us," compressed to two sentences. |
| "How many universities did you apply to? Which admitted you?" | Consistency and credibility of the plan. Honest numbers; officers can often see application patterns. |
| "Why study in the US instead of your home country?" | A concrete academic reason (program, research, curriculum) — not "the US is better." |
| "What will you study, and what does it lead to?" | Whether the major connects to a plausible career, ideally one anchored at home. |
| "Who is paying for your education?" | Funding adequacy and consistency with the financial documents behind the I-20. Name the sponsor and the source plainly. |
| "What does your sponsor do? What is their income?" | Credibility of the funding claim. Approximate but honest figures matching the paperwork. |
| "What are your plans after graduation?" | The 214(b) core: intent to return. Concrete ties — family, career plans, obligations at home — carry the answer. |
| "Do you have relatives in the United States?" | Immigration-intent signal. Answer truthfully; lying here is far more damaging than any honest answer. |
| "Will you work while studying?" | Knowledge of F-1 rules: on-campus work and authorized training (e.g., OPT/CPT) are the safe, truthful frame — "my sponsor covers my costs" plus awareness of the rules. |
| "What are your test scores / academic results?" | Basic academic credibility for the admitted program; know the numbers without notes. |

Prep notes for the visa variant:

- Practice **brevity**: consular answers are 1–3 sentences, which feels rude
  to students trained for conversational college interviews. Run a separate
  short-form mock (`mock-interviewer` agent, interview type "F-1 visa").
- Never coach a student to claim ties or plans they do not have — a refused
  visa can typically be reapplied for; a misrepresentation finding follows
  them permanently.
- Documents in hand, but volunteered only when asked: passport, I-20, DS-160
  confirmation, SEVIS fee receipt, admission letter, financial evidence.
