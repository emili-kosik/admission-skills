# Activities craft ŌĆö writing the 150-character description

The Common App description field is 150 characters including spaces. That is not
an essay, a paragraph, or even a full sentence ŌĆö it is a compressed line of
evidence. The craft below is about spending every character on facts a reader
can weigh. (Verify current field limits inside the application; they have been
stable for years.)

One rule above all: **everything must be true and student-supplied.** The job is
compression, not decoration. If a number is a guess, ask the student; if a title
is aspirational, cut it.

## The verb-first formula

```
[Action verb] + [what, specifically] + [scale or number] + [outcome or change]
```

- Start with a verb. Never "I" (the whole application is about the student),
  never "Responsible for", never "Member of".
- Sentence fragments are expected. Drop articles where they read fine.
- Chain 2-3 clauses with semicolons rather than writing one vague sentence.
- Standard abbreviations are fine and readers expect them: `hrs/wk`, `~`, `&`,
  `2x/wk`, `$1.2k`, `ŌåÆ` (as in "grew 8ŌåÆ31 members").
- Don't repeat the other two fields. Position and organization print right next
  to the description ŌĆö "Played varsity soccer for the varsity soccer team"
  wastes ~40 characters saying nothing new.

## Quantification patterns

A number turns a claim into evidence. The usual sources:

| Pattern | Example |
|---|---|
| People reached or led | `taught 30 fourth-graders`, `led 12-student crew` |
| Money raised or managed | `raised $2,400 for shelter`, `managed $800 budget` |
| Frequency and time | `15 hrs/wk`, `weekly since 9th grade`, `3 seasons` |
| Output produced | `published 14 articles`, `built 6 sets`, `shipped app w/ 900 downloads` |
| Growth caused | `grew club 8ŌåÆ31 members`, `doubled meet attendance` |
| Scale of context | `school of 2,100`, `district's only bilingual tutor` |
| Rank or selection | `1 of 6 chosen statewide`, `first chair, 2 yrs` |

Two or three numbers per line is the sweet spot. Zero numbers usually means the
line is still abstract; five numbers reads like a spreadsheet.

## Before / after

**1. The generic club member**

> *Before (136 chars, says nothing):* Member of the Environmental Club. We did
> many activities to help the environment and raise awareness about climate
> change in our school.

> *After (136 chars):* Led 12-student stream-cleanup crew; logged 40 bags of
> litter over 6 outings; won $500 district grant to install 3 water refill
> stations.

Same student, same club. The before spends its budget on category words
("activities", "awareness"); the after spends it on what happened.

**2. The athlete**

> *Before (131 chars):* Varsity soccer player. Attended all practices and
> games. Learned teamwork, leadership, and time management through this
> experience.

> *After (117 chars):* Started 34 varsity games at center back; captained
> defense senior yr; ran preseason fitness plan later adopted by JV.

"Learned teamwork, leadership, and time management" is the single most common
wasted clause in the format. Lessons learned belong nowhere on this list ŌĆö
readers infer them from what the student did.

**3. The part-time job**

> *Before (112 chars):* Worked as a cashier at a grocery store.
> Responsibilities included handling money, customer service and stocking.

> *After (111 chars):* Worked 15 hrs/wk to help with family expenses; trained
> 4 new hires; promoted to weekend shift lead in 8 months.

A job is a strong activity; "responsibilities included" is a weak frame. Show
trust earned (training others, promotion) and, if the student is comfortable,
why the job matters.

**4. The family responsibility**

> *Before (53 chars):* Helped take care of my younger siblings after school.

> *After (123 chars):* Primary after-school caregiver for 2 siblings (ages 6 &
> 9): pickup, homework help, dinner ŌĆö 20 hrs/wk while parents worked.

Caregiving *is* an activity, and admissions readers say so explicitly. The
after-version quantifies the load so the reader can weigh it against a peer's
club schedule.

**5. The research project**

> *Before (125 chars):* Did a research project about machine learning with a
> professor at a local university and learned a lot about the field of AI.

> *After (127 chars):* Built Python pipeline classifying 10k+ birdsong clips
> (87% accuracy) in univ. ecology lab; presented poster at state symposium.

"Did a project" ŌåÆ what was built, at what scale, with what result, shown where.

## The position field (50 chars) and organization field (100 chars)

- Position: the real title, plainly ŌĆö `Captain`, `Section Editor`, `Shift Lead`,
  `Volunteer Tutor`. If there was no title, describe the role: `Founding Member`,
  `Lead Programmer`. Progression fits too: `Member (9-10), Treasurer (11-12)`.
- Organization: full name plus a gloss if the name is opaque:
  `Ocean Bowl ŌĆö competitive marine-science quiz team`.

## Common mistakes

1. **Title-case stuffing.** Cramming `Founder, President, CEO & Executive
   Director` into the position field for a three-person club. Inflated titles
   are a known pattern and readers discount them; one honest title with evidence
   in the description outweighs four grand ones.
2. **Listing, not showing.** `Meetings, fundraisers, events, volunteering` is a
   category list. Pick the one or two concrete things with numbers attached.
3. **Repeating the essay.** If the personal essay is about debate, the debate
   description should carry *different* facts (record, tournaments, roles), not
   a 150-character summary of the essay. Each application component should add
   new information. The reverse also holds: an "activity elaboration"
   supplement should zoom into one moment, not re-list this entry.
4. **Filler phrases.** `Responsible for`, `helped with`, `participated in`,
   `various`, `learned valuable lessons`, `passionate about` ŌĆö each costs
   15-25 characters and adds zero evidence. Delete on sight.
5. **Unexplained acronyms.** `Placed 3rd at HOSA SLC` means nothing to a reader
   outside the circuit. Spell out or gloss: `HOSA (health-science org) state
   conference`.
6. **Implausible arithmetic.** Hours/week x weeks/year across ten activities
   sometimes exceeds the waking year. Readers add it up; so should you, before
   they do.
7. **Burying the lead.** The most meaningful activity sits in slot 5 because
   the student sorted by "impressiveness" of the organization. Sort by the
   student's actual depth and impact ŌĆö slot 1 sets the frame for the whole list.
8. **Padding to ten.** A one-meeting club from 9th grade dilutes the list.
   Eight strong entries beat ten where two are trivia.

## Honors lines (the 5 slots)

The same compression logic, smaller canvas: the honor's real name, plus a gloss
if selectivity is invisible ŌĆö `Regional Science Fair, 1st in physics (of 214
entrants)`. Order national ŌåÆ state/regional ŌåÆ school. If an "honor" is really an
activity outcome (team won states), it usually works harder as a number inside
that activity's description.

## Working checklist for each description

- [ ] Starts with an action verb, no "I"
- [ ] 1-3 numbers (people, money, time, output, growth, rank)
- [ ] Says something the position + organization fields don't already say
- [ ] No filler phrases, no lessons-learned clause
- [ ] Acronyms glossed
- [ ] Character count stated and Ōēż150 including spaces
- [ ] Every fact confirmed by the student
