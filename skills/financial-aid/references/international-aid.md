# Aid for international students — a different game with different rules

Almost everything US families are told about financial aid assumes US
citizenship. For an international applicant (not a US citizen or permanent
resident), start from these facts and label them clearly when advising.

## What does not apply

- **No FAFSA, no federal aid.** Pell Grants, federal loans, and work-study are
  for US citizens and eligible noncitizens only. (A small set of statuses —
  e.g., permanent residents, certain refugees/asylees — count as eligible
  noncitizens; check studentaid.gov if the status is ambiguous.)
- **State aid** almost never applies, and public universities typically charge
  internationals out-of-state or higher rates with little discount.
- What remains: **the college's own institutional aid**, merit scholarships,
  and funding from home (family, home-country government, external sponsors).

## The forms internationals actually file

There is no single form. Each college's aid page names one of:

- the **CSS Profile** (many private colleges use it for internationals —
  confirm via the participating-school search, and see `css-profile.md`),
- the **ISFAA** (International Student Financial Aid Application, a paper/PDF
  form some colleges prefer), or
- the college's **own institutional form**.

Build a per-college checklist in the tracker; the mix is different at every
school on the list.

## Need-aware: the strategic reality no one states plainly

Most US colleges are **need-aware for international applicants**: whether and
how much aid you request can affect the admission decision itself. That
produces two honest consequences:

1. **Decide about aid before applying, not after.** At most colleges, an
   international student admitted as "no need" typically cannot ask for
   institutional aid in later years — verify each college's stated policy
   before relying on this either way.
2. **Never understate need to look stronger.** Being admitted without the
   money to attend helps no one, and the visa process (below) will surface the
   truth anyway.

A small number of US colleges state that they are **need-blind and
full-need-met for international applicants**. The list is short, it changes,
and secondhand lists online are chronically stale — **verify each college's
own stated policy on its admissions/aid pages**; do not rely on any list,
including anything this plugin could generate from memory.

## Certification of finances — and the I-20 connection

Colleges require documentation that the family can pay its share: a
**certification/declaration of finances** form plus **bank statements** and,
if someone else is paying, a **sponsor letter**. Know what this is really for:

- The same documentation is what the college's international office uses to
  issue the **Form I-20**, the document that anchors the F-1 visa. Aid
  packaging and visa eligibility draw on one pool of evidence.
- Therefore the numbers must be **consistent everywhere**: the aid
  application, the certification of finances, and the bank statements should
  tell one coherent story. A generous aid award reduces the funds the family
  must show for the I-20.
- Expect to show liquid funds covering roughly the first year's total cost
  minus any awarded aid — the exact figure is set by each college; take it
  from the college's own I-20/financial-certification instructions.

For I-20 timing, the SEVIS fee, and the visa interview itself, route to the
`international` skill — this file covers only the money.

## Practical playbook

1. Junior year: build the college list with aid policy as a **first-class
   filter** — for internationals, affordability is an admissions variable,
   not an afterthought. Route to `college-list` with this constraint.
2. For each candidate college, record on its tracker checklist: aid policy
   for internationals (need-blind / need-aware / no aid), which form it wants,
   and its deadline.
3. Prepare financial documents once, early: tax-equivalents or income
   statements, 3–6 months of bank statements, sponsor letters. Translations
   and currency conversions take time.
4. Pursue merit scholarships open to internationals (`scholarships` skill) and
   home-country funding — EducationUSA advising centers (US State Department)
   are a free, legitimate resource in most countries.
5. In April, compare offers with `award-letters.md` as usual — plus one extra
   line per college: funds the family must certify for the I-20.
