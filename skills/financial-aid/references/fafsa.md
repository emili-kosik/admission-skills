# FAFSA — filing it right, on time, as a family

The Free Application for Federal Student Aid (https://studentaid.gov/h/apply-for-aid/fafsa)
is the gateway form: federal Pell Grants, federal student loans, and work-study
all flow from it, and most states and many colleges reuse it for their own
need-based and even merit money. It is free — anyone charging to "file your
FAFSA" is a scam. It opens **October 1** for the following academic year
(verify the current cycle's opening on studentaid.gov — it has slipped in past
cycles).

## Why everyone files, even "we won't qualify" families

- Federal unsubsidized loans and work-study do not depend on need — but they
  require a FAFSA.
- Many colleges and states require a FAFSA on file before releasing *merit*
  scholarships.
- Circumstances change (job loss, a second child in college); a filed FAFSA is
  the prerequisite for a mid-year professional-judgment review.

## Step 1 — FSA IDs, a week before you file

Every **contributor** needs their own FSA ID (account at studentaid.gov):

- The **student** creates one with their own email and phone.
- Each required **parent contributor** creates their own — a contributor is
  anyone whose information must go on the form: typically one or both parents,
  or a parent and stepparent. Spouses filing joint taxes may be covered by one
  contributor; the form's invitation flow tells you exactly who it needs.
- For divorced or separated parents, the parent on the FAFSA is the one who
  provided **more financial support** in the past year — not the one the
  student lives with. Confirm the current rule on studentaid.gov.
- A parent without a Social Security number can still create an FSA ID; the
  site has an identity-verification path for this case.

Create the IDs **at least a week before filing**: identity matching with the
Social Security Administration typically takes a few days, and a pending FSA
ID blocks the whole form. Use real personal emails (not the student's school
email, which dies at graduation), and store the credentials somewhere the
family can find in January.

## Step 2 — consent, taxes, and the SAI (what replaced the EFC)

- Every contributor must **consent to the IRS data exchange** — the form pulls
  federal tax information directly. No consent, no aid, full stop.
- The form uses **prior-prior year** taxes (for fall 2027 enrollment, 2025
  taxes). There is nothing to optimize at filing time; the return already
  exists.
- The result is the **Student Aid Index (SAI)**, which replaced the EFC. It is
  an eligibility index, not "what you will pay": it can go as low as −1500,
  and unlike the old EFC it is *not* divided among siblings in college at the
  same time. Colleges subtract SAI from their cost of attendance to size
  need-based aid.

## Step 3 — deadlines: three layers, only one is safe to state from memory

| Layer | When | Where to check |
|---|---|---|
| **Federal** | Late — typically June 30 after the academic year ends. Never the binding constraint. | https://studentaid.gov/apply-for-aid/fafsa/fafsa-deadlines |
| **State** | Varies wildly; some are early winter, several are **"as soon as possible after Oct 1 — first come, first served."** | The same live studentaid.gov page, state dropdown — **always** answer state-deadline questions from that page, never from memory or this file |
| **College priority** | Set per college; typically aligns with the application round (Nov for ED/EA, Jan–Feb for RD) | Each college's financial aid page |

The operating rule: **file within the first weeks of October**. Meeting the
earliest priority deadline on the family's list automatically meets the rest.
Add each college's aid priority date to its tracker checklist.

## After filing

- The student receives a **FAFSA Submission Summary** with the SAI; colleges
  listed on the form receive the data directly.
- Review the summary for errors; corrections are made by logging back in.
  Adding colleges later is allowed — do it as the list changes.
- Aid offers arrive **with or after admission decisions**, not when you file.
- The FAFSA is **annual** — refile every fall the student is enrolled.

## Who cannot file

FAFSA is for US citizens and eligible noncitizens (e.g., permanent residents).
Most international students cannot file — their path is institutional aid via
the CSS Profile or a college's own forms. Read `international-aid.md`.
