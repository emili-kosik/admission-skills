# Brainstorm exercises — the Excavate phase

Socratic interviews about the student's real life. Capture their answers
**verbatim** in `essays/brainstorm/<topic>.md` — the student's own phrasing is
often the voice the essay needs; paraphrasing it into polished prose destroys
the evidence and edges toward authorship (E2/E3).

## Question families

**Moments** — force specificity past the résumé layer:
- "Walk me through a specific Tuesday when this activity mattered."
- "What's the last thing that happened in <activity> that you told someone
  about voluntarily?"
- "Where were you standing? Who else was there? What did it smell like?"

**Contradictions** — essays live where the simple story breaks:
- "When did you act against your own advice?"
- "What do you do well that you don't enjoy? Enjoy that you're bad at?"
- "What would your teammates be surprised to learn you think about <activity>?"

**Stakes** — reveal what actually matters:
- "What would you have lost if it had gone the other way?"
- "What did this cost you — time, friendships, certainty?"

**Change** — growth prompts need a before and an after:
- "What did you believe before that you don't now?"
- "Who did you argue with about this, and what did they get right?"

**Texture** — the details only this student can supply:
- "What object would you keep from this experience?"
- "What's a sentence someone actually said during it that stuck with you?"

## Session shape (10–20 minutes)

1. Pick one experience the student named — never one you suggested (E2).
2. 4–6 questions from different families; follow the energy, not the list.
3. Read back the 3 most alive verbatim lines you heard; ask "which of these
   surprises you?"
4. Save the notes file: raw Q&A, then a "material inventory" section listing
   moments/objects/lines with no evaluation.
5. Map to prompts (Choose phase) only after the material exists — topic-first
   beats prompt-first.

## What this phase never does

No thesis statements, no "your angle could be…", no sample openings, no
outline "just to organize" (outlining is banned — Caltech names it
explicitly, and the floor applies everywhere). The bridge from material to
draft is the student's to build.
