# English proficiency tests — TOEFL, IELTS, Duolingo

For international applicants (and some domestic applicants schooled outside
English-medium systems), most US colleges require proof of English
proficiency **in addition to** any SAT/ACT decision. This is a separate
requirement with its own calendar — the SAT/ACT regimes (required/optional/
free) do not apply to it.

## Who needs one

Typically required when English is neither the student's native language nor
the language of instruction at their school. Each college defines its own
trigger (citizenship, schooling language, years in an English-medium
system), so check the **international applicants** page of every school on
the list — not the general testing page.

## The three main tests

| | TOEFL iBT | IELTS Academic | Duolingo English Test (DET) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Format | test center or Home Edition; ~2 hours | test center (computer or paper); ~2¾ hours, incl. live speaking interview | fully online at home; ~1 hour |
| Results | typically ~3–4 days to your account | typically 1–5 days (computer), ~13 days (paper) | typically ~2 days |
| Official delivery to colleges | allow extra days–weeks for sending and college processing | allow extra days–weeks | electronic sends, typically fast and free per college |
| Acceptance | near-universal in the US | near-universal | broad and growing, but **narrower — confirm each college accepts it** |
| Cost | varies by country — see ets.org/toefl | varies by country — see ielts.org | typically the cheapest of the three — see englishtest.duolingo.com |

Sources: [ets.org/toefl](https://www.ets.org/toefl), [ielts.org](https://ielts.org),
[englishtest.duolingo.com](https://englishtest.duolingo.com). Fees, formats,
and delivery times change — verify on these pages before the family books
anything.

Choosing: if every school on the list accepts the DET, it is the cheapest
and most convenient option; TOEFL/IELTS are the safe default for lists that
aren't finalized. There is no admissions advantage to one test over another
at colleges that accept all three.

## Timing — the delivery gap is the trap

Colleges need the score **in hand**, not merely taken. Between result
release and official delivery/processing, budget two to three weeks
end-to-end. Rules of thumb:

- **Nov 1 ED/EA deadlines → test by September, October at the latest.**
- **Jan 1–15 RD deadlines → test by October–November.**
- Leave room for one retake: a first sitting in late spring or summer of
  11th grade means a senior-September retake is a comfort, not a crisis.
- Scores are typically valid for **two years** — an 11th-grade score
  usually covers the whole application cycle. Verify validity terms on the
  test's own site.

## Minimums vary per college

Each college publishes its own minimum (or "recommended") scores, and they
differ widely. As an *illustrative pattern only* — never a substitute for
the college's published number:

- The most selective colleges typically look for TOEFL 100+, IELTS 7.0+,
  DET 120+.
- Many other institutions typically publish minimums around TOEFL 80–90,
  IELTS 6.0–6.5, DET 105–115.

Some programs also set per-section minimums (speaking is the common one).
Verify every number on each college's international admissions page, and
record the highest minimum on the list as the student's real target.

## Waivers — common patterns, always per-college

Many colleges waive the proficiency requirement for students who:

- completed several years (typically 3–4) of schooling where English is the
  language of instruction;
- are citizens/long-term residents of designated English-speaking countries;
- earned a strong SAT EBRW or ACT English score above the college's
  published floor.

Waiver terms are among the least standardized policies in admissions — some
schools grant them automatically, some require a counselor letter or a
formal request. Confirm on each college's international page before skipping
the test; a wrongly assumed waiver discovered in December is very hard to
fix.
